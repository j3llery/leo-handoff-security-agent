## Limitations of AI-Assisted Analysis

### 1. What the Analysis Did Well

**Quantified uncertainty rather than reporting point estimates alone.** The pipeline attached a 95% confidence interval to the coefficient of variation ([0.101, 0.267]) and a half-width of ±0.7s to the mean interval estimate. This is genuinely sound because it exposes — rather than hides — the fact that the central CV estimate of 0.147 is not precise enough to support a clean classification. Many analyses would have reported CV = 0.147 and stopped; computing the interval is what made the threshold problem visible at all.

**Tested the Poisson (memoryless) null hypothesis explicitly.** The z-score of 51.37 against a Poisson process is a decisive rejection, and this matters: it establishes that the handoff intervals are *not* random arrivals but carry genuine temporal structure (regularity), which is the precondition for any predictability claim. This is a meaningful, defensible result independent of the weaker periodicity findings.

**Propagated timing uncertainty into the attack-probability estimate.** The p_hit_per_prediction of 0.0595 was derived by combining the ±2.16s jitter with the 0.323s disruption window (exploitable fraction 0.0219), rather than asserting an attack succeeds deterministically. The reasoning chain — 244 handoffs/hour → ~14.5 expected hits — is internally consistent arithmetic that correctly frames the attack as probabilistic and low-yield per attempt.

**Self-reported its own circularity and FFT failure.** The diagnostics explicitly flag the synthetic-data circularity (std recovery ratio 0.865), the CI straddling the threshold, and the 60.8% FFT period error. Documenting known failure modes in machine-readable notes is better practice than silently omitting them.

### 2. What the Analysis Did Poorly or Uncertainly

**The validation is circular by construction.** The data is synthetic ("data_source": "synthetic"), generated from Starlink Gen1 parameters that the analysis then "confirms." The std recovery ratio of 0.865 — measured_std divided by the generator's own injected jitter — being close to 1.0 means the pipeline is largely recovering its own input assumptions. No finding here is evidence about real Starlink behavior; it is evidence that the estimator can approximately invert its own generator. Every downstream number (CV, attack probability, disruption window) inherits this circularity.

**The headline "highly predictable" classification is not supported by the data.** That label requires CV < 0.20, but the 95% CI for CV is [0.101, 0.267]. The interval straddles the threshold — the upper bound (0.267) lands firmly in "unpredictable" territory. The point estimate (0.147) was used as if it were the truth while the interval was ignored. The honest conclusion is that the data cannot distinguish "highly predictable" from "moderately unpredictable."

**The sample size is too small for the claims made.** With only 39 intervals (40 handoffs over 600s), the CI half-width on the mean is ±0.7s on a 14.74s mean — roughly 5% — but the CV interval width (0.166) is more than the point estimate's distance to the threshold. Stable CV estimation needs substantially more intervals; ten minutes of data is inadequate to characterize a process the analysis simultaneously claims produces 244 handoffs/hour.

**The FFT analysis failed and was quietly bypassed.** The FFT returned a dominant period of 5.77s against an expected ~14.74s — a 60.8% error — carrying only 0.43% of total band power. The diagnostics attribute this to a 10s detrending window attenuating the ~1/15 Hz signal of interest, which is a plausible methodological self-diagnosis. But the failure was *not corrected*: interval-level statistics were substituted without reconciling the discrepancy. A spectral method that locates the dominant period off by more than half its true value, on data engineered to be periodic, is a method that should not be trusted on real data — and its 0.43% band power directly contradicts the "highly predictable" framing.

**The disruption window has no ground truth.** The mean disruption window of 0.323s (std 0.053s) drives the entire attack-feasibility calculation, yet there is no independent validation of how it was measured or whether 0.32s reflects real handoff-induced RTT disruption versus a generator parameter. Since exploitable_fraction_of_cycle (0.0219) and therefore p_hit (0.0595) scale directly with this number, an unvalidated disruption window means the attack probability is unfalsifiable.

**The threat-scenario mechanism is asserted, not demonstrated.** The claim that an attacker "launching within the 0.32s disruption window" achieves a successful attack assumes, without evidence, that (a) handoff timing is externally observable, (b) the disruption window is exploitable rather than merely a latency blip, and (c) a 0.32s window is operationally actionable. None of these mechanistic links is tested; "~15 successful attack windows per hour" is an arithmetic projection from assumptions, not a measured attack success rate.

### 3. Limitations of This Approach

**Data requirements.** A rigorous version requires real Starlink telemetry — captured RTT/loss traces or handoff logs across many hours, multiple terminals, varied geographic locations, and varied load and weather conditions. Ten minutes of synthetic data (6000 samples, 40 handoffs) cannot characterize a system whose behavior varies with constellation geometry and time of day. The CV interval alone implies hundreds of handoff intervals are needed before classification is meaningful.

**Experimental validation for attack feasibility.** The attack claims demand an actual testbed: a real terminal, a real or controlled adversary attempting to time an intervention to a predicted handoff, and a measured success rate. Only a measured p_hit — not one computed from a self-generated jitter and an unvalidated disruption window — can support the claim that handoff timing is exploitable. The mechanism connecting a 0.32s disruption to an actual security impact must itself be demonstrated.

**Why synthetic findings cannot be published as empirical results.** Because the data was generated from the same parameters the analysis recovers (std recovery ratio 0.865), the results describe the generator, not Starlink. Presenting any of these numbers — the 14.74s mean, the 0.147 CV, the 6.0% hit probability — as empirical findings about a real system would be misrepresentation. They are at most consistency checks on the simulation.

**Peer review for novel threat claims.** A claim that a deployed satellite network has a predictable, exploitable handoff vulnerability is a significant security assertion requiring independent replication on real data, disclosure to the vendor, scrutiny of the threat model's realism (attacker observability, timing precision, actionable window), and reviewers with domain expertise in LEO networking. None of the present results clear that bar.

**Summary:** This work is a research proposal and methodology prototype, not a set of validated findings. It demonstrates a coherent analytical pipeline — Poisson rejection (z = 51.37), uncertainty quantification (CV CI [0.101, 0.267]), and a probabilistic attack model — and it honestly self-documents its own failure modes. But every substantive number derives from synthetic data the analysis then "confirms," the FFT method demonstrably failed (60.8% period error, 0.43% band power), the central "highly predictable" classification is contradicted by its own confidence interval, and the attack-feasibility figures rest on an unvalidated disruption window and untested mechanistic assumptions. It establishes a framework worth applying to real telemetry; it establishes nothing about Starlink security as it stands.
